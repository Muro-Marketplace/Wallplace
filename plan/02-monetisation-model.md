# Section 2: Monetisation Model Design

## Revenue Architecture Overview

Wallspace has six potential revenue streams. Not all should be activated at launch. The model below separates what generates revenue in v1 from what should wait for v2 and what should be avoided early.

---

## V1 Revenue Streams

### Primary: Artist Monthly Membership (Recurring)

This is the core revenue engine from day one.

| Tier | Monthly | Annual (15% discount) | Target Segment |
|------|---------|----------------------|----------------|
| Founding | £29/mo | £296/yr (£24.67/mo) | Early adopters, limited to first 50 artists |
| Core | £49/mo | £499/yr (£41.58/mo) | Standard professional artists/photographers |
| Premium | £89/mo | £899/yr (£74.92/mo) | High-output artists wanting maximum placement |

**Revenue modelling at launch (30-50 artists):**

Conservative (30 artists, weighted toward Founding):
- 20 Founding @ £29 = £580/mo
- 8 Core @ £49 = £392/mo
- 2 Premium @ £89 = £178/mo
- **Total: £1,150/mo (£13,800/yr)**

Target (50 artists, healthier mix):
- 20 Founding @ £29 = £580/mo
- 20 Core @ £49 = £980/mo
- 10 Premium @ £89 = £890/mo
- **Total: £2,450/mo (£29,400/yr)**

At 200 artists (12-18 month target):
- 30 Founding @ £29 = £870/mo
- 110 Core @ £49 = £5,390/mo
- 60 Premium @ £89 = £5,340/mo
- **Total: £11,600/mo (£139,200/yr)**

**Why this works as primary revenue:**
- Predictable, recurring, independent of sales velocity
- Scales linearly with artist acquisition
- Provides quality filter (paying artists are serious artists)
- Funds operations before commission revenue matures
- Annual plans reduce churn and improve cash flow

**Key principle:** Membership must deliver concrete value independent of sales. Artists are paying for placement, visibility, and professional presentation -- not for a promise that their work might sell.

---

### Secondary: Commission on Sales (Variable)

Two commission structures based on buyer type:

| Sale Type | Commission | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Customer purchase (via venue QR/link) | 20% | Customer discovers art in venue, buys through Wallspace. Full discovery attribution. |
| Direct venue purchase | 15% | Venue buys art for permanent display. Lower commission because venue is the channel partner, not just the discovery point. |

**Example unit economics on a £200 print sale (customer purchase):**

- Sale price: £200
- Wallspace commission (20%): £40
- Print production cost (if Wallspace handles): £25-40
- Artist receives: £120-135
- Venue referral bonus (if applicable): £0-10

**Example unit economics on a £500 original (venue purchase):**

- Sale price: £500
- Wallspace commission (15%): £75
- Artist receives: £425
- No production cost (original work)

**Revenue modelling on commission:**

Conservative (5 sales/month average in first 6 months):
- Average sale value: £180
- Average commission: £34 (blended ~19%)
- **Commission revenue: £170/mo**

Target (15 sales/month at 12 months):
- Average sale value: £200
- Average commission: £38
- **Commission revenue: £570/mo**

Growth (40 sales/month at 18-24 months):
- Average sale value: £220
- Average commission: £42
- **Commission revenue: £1,680/mo**

**Key principle:** Commission revenue is upside, not the foundation. Do not build the financial model around commission projections. Treat it as bonus revenue that improves unit economics over time.

## V2 Revenue Streams (Not at Launch)

These should be designed but not activated until v1 is stable and the core model is proven. Target activation: 6-12 months after launch.

### Margin on Print Production and Framing

Once Wallspace has volume, negotiate preferred rates with print and framing suppliers and add a margin.

- Artist uploads digital file
- Wallspace handles print production and framing through partner suppliers
- Wallspace charges artist a marked-up rate (15-25% margin on production cost)
- This becomes a convenience service that increases stickiness

**Example:**
- Print + frame cost from supplier: £35
- Wallspace charges artist: £45
- Margin: £10 per unit

At 50 prints/month: £500/mo additional revenue.

**Why wait:** Requires supplier relationships, quality control processes, and enough volume to negotiate rates. Adding this too early adds operational complexity without meaningful revenue.

### Venue Premium Tiers

Once venues are habituated to the free service, introduce optional paid tiers:

| Venue Tier | Price | Includes |
|-----------|-------|----------|
| Free (Core) | £0/mo | Curated art rotation, basic installation, standard rotation schedule |
| Venue Plus | £49/mo | Priority curation, custom style matching, featured venue on Wallspace site, monthly rotation |
| Venue Partner | £149/mo | Dedicated curator, event-ready art selection, branded gallery wall, bi-weekly rotation, sales reporting |

**Why wait:** Venues must first experience the value of the free tier. Introducing paid tiers too early poisons the "it's free" pitch that makes venue acquisition easy. Wait until venues are asking for more before offering premium.

### Rotation Programme Fees

Charge artists a per-rotation fee for swapping work between venues.

- Standard rotation (quarterly): included in membership
- Accelerated rotation (monthly): £15/rotation
- On-demand rotation: £25/rotation

**Why wait:** Rotation logistics need to be proven operationally before monetising them. In v1, the founder handles rotation manually. Adding fees before the process is smooth creates friction.

## V3 Revenue Streams (12-24 Months)

### White-Glove Service for Hotels, Offices, and Corporate Groups

Fully managed art programmes for larger commercial clients:

- Art sourcing and curation for multi-room/multi-floor installations
- Custom framing and branding
- Scheduled rotation programmes
- Damage/insurance management
- Dedicated account management

**Pricing:** Project-based (£2,000-£10,000 per installation) plus ongoing management fees (£200-£500/month per site).

**Why wait:** This is a different sales motion (B2B, longer cycles, procurement processes) that requires credibility, a portfolio of venue installations, and operational maturity. Do not distract from the core marketplace in v1.

### Bulk Procurement and Multi-Site Packages

For restaurant groups, co-working chains, and hospitality brands with multiple locations:

- Volume discounts on curation fees
- Centralised art programme management
- Cross-site rotation
- Brand-aligned art selection

**Pricing:** Custom, based on number of sites and rotation frequency. Typically £100-£300/month per site with 12-month contracts.

### Curation Fees for Larger Buyers

For interior designers, property developers, or private collectors who discover artists through Wallspace venues:

- Curation consultation: £150-£300 per session
- Sourcing commission: 10-15% on procurement value
- Project management for large installations: hourly or project-based

## What to Avoid Early

### Advertising or Sponsored Placement

Do not sell advertising space or "featured artist" slots funded by brands. This undermines curation integrity and confuses the value proposition. Wallspace is a curated channel, not an ad network.

### Transaction Fees on Artist-to-Buyer Direct Sales

If an artist sells directly to a buyer they met through a venue (bypassing Wallspace), do not try to capture a fee. This is unenforceable, creates resentment, and the revenue is marginal. Instead, make the Wallspace sales infrastructure so convenient that artists prefer to use it.

### Venue Charging in v1

Absolutely do not charge venues anything in v1. The entire model depends on frictionless venue acquisition. Even a small fee (£10/month) would dramatically increase objection rates and slow growth.

### Complex Licensing or Royalty Models

Do not introduce licensing fees, reproduction rights, or royalty structures. These add legal complexity, confuse artists, and generate minimal revenue at early scale. Keep the model simple: membership + commission.

### Freemium Artist Tier

Do not offer a free artist tier. A free tier attracts low-quality submissions, undermines the quality filter, devalues paid memberships, and creates a support burden without revenue. The lowest tier is £29/month. If an artist will not pay £29/month, they are not the target customer.

## Revenue Mix Summary

| Phase | Primary Revenue | Secondary Revenue | Tertiary Revenue |
|-------|----------------|-------------------|------------------|
| V1 (Launch - Month 6) | Artist membership (£1,150-£2,450/mo) | Commission on sales (£170-£570/mo) | None |
| V2 (Month 6-12) | Artist membership (scaling) | Commission on sales (scaling) | Print/frame margin, venue premium tiers |
| V3 (Month 12-24) | Artist membership + venue premium | Commission + production margin | White-glove, bulk, curation fees |

**Target blended revenue at 12 months:** £8,000-£12,000/month
**Target blended revenue at 24 months:** £20,000-£35,000/month

---

## Best Mix of Recurring Revenue, Quality Filtering, and Scalability

The recommended model optimises for three things simultaneously:

1. **Recurring revenue:** Artist membership provides predictable monthly income that funds operations independent of sales velocity. Annual plans improve cash flow further.

2. **Quality filtering:** The membership paywall is the first quality gate. The application/curation process is the second. Together they ensure only professional, commercially viable work enters the platform.

3. **Scalability:** The model scales by adding more artists (revenue grows linearly) and more venues (placement capacity grows, making artist membership more valuable, enabling price increases or higher-tier adoption). Commission revenue scales with both.

The critical ratio to monitor is **membership revenue vs. operational cost per artist.** As long as the average revenue per artist (membership + attributed commission) exceeds the cost to serve that artist (curation time, placement logistics, sales support), the model scales profitably.

At launch, the cost per artist will be high (founder-operated, manual processes). The path to profitability is: systematise operations, increase venue density (reducing logistics cost per placement), and shift the artist mix toward higher tiers as the value proposition strengthens.
