# Section 9: Unit Economics and Pricing

## 9.1 Pricing Structure: Lean V1

### 9.1.1 Artist Membership Pricing

| | Founding | Core | Premium |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Monthly** | £29/mo | £49/mo | £89/mo |
| **Annual** | £290/yr (£24.17/mo effective) | £490/yr (£40.83/mo effective) | £890/yr (£74.17/mo effective) |
| **Annual saving** | £58 (2 months free) | £98 (2 months free) | £178 (2 months free) |
| **Availability** | First 50 artists only | Open | Open |
| **Works listed** | Up to 5 | Up to 15 | Unlimited |
| **Venue matching** | Standard | Priority | Priority + dedicated manager |
| **Analytics** | Basic | Standard | Premium |
| **Photography support** | Style guide | Style guide + review | 1x annual pro shoot |
| **Exhibition events** | Community access | Community access | Event hosting support |

**Pricing rationale:**

- **£29 Founding tier** is priced below the psychological £30 barrier. It is deliberately cheap to attract the first cohort and create urgency ("limited to 50 artists"). The founding tier is a customer acquisition tool, not a margin driver. At £29/mo, an artist is spending less than a single print sale would earn them. It should feel like an obvious yes.

- **£49 Core tier** is the long-term workhorse tier. It should represent the majority of memberships once the founding tier closes. At £49/mo (£588/yr), it is less than a single original artwork sale for most photographers. The value proposition is clear: access to a curated exhibition network in high-footfall venues for less than the cost of a single gallery submission fee.

- **£89 Premium tier** is the high-touch tier for serious artists who want maximum exposure and support. The dedicated account manager and annual photography session justify the premium. This tier also serves as a price anchor that makes Core feel affordable.

**Recommendations:**

- **No setup fees.** Setup fees create friction at the exact moment you want frictionless onboarding. The membership itself serves as a commitment signal.
- **No application fees.** Charging artists to apply creates a pay-to-play perception that damages brand trust. Applications should be free; membership is the monetisation point.
- **Annual billing incentive.** Push annual billing in onboarding. Annual subscribers have dramatically lower churn and provide predictable cash flow. The 2-months-free incentive is standard and effective.
- **Founding tier lock-in.** Founding members keep their £29/mo rate for as long as they maintain continuous membership. This rewards early adopters and creates word-of-mouth ("I got in at the founding rate").

### 9.1.2 Commission Structure

| Sale Type | Commission | Artist Receives | Example (£500 artwork) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Customer purchase via venue** | 20% | 80% | Wallspace: £100, Artist: £400 |
| **Venue direct purchase** | 15% | 85% | Wallspace: £75, Artist: £425 |

**Commission rationale:**

- **20% for customer sales** is justified because Wallspace created the entire demand chain: curated the work, placed it in the venue, provided the information card, processed the payment. The artist did nothing beyond creating the work and saying yes to placement. Compared to gallery (50%), Saatchi Art (35%), and Artfinder (30-33%), 20% is extremely competitive.

- **15% for venue purchases** is lower because the venue is an existing Wallspace relationship. The venue is buying work already displayed in their space. Lower commission encourages venue purchases, which are simpler to process (no customer delivery needed).

- **No commission on work sold outside Wallspace.** Artists are free to sell their work through other channels. Wallspace only takes commission on sales that flow through the Wallspace placement and payment system.

### 9.3.3 Per-Sale Economics

| Component | Customer Sale (£350 artwork) | Venue Sale (£350 artwork) |
|---|---|---|
| **Sale price** | £350 | £350 |
| **Commission (20% / 15%)** | £70 | £52.50 |

**Key insight:** Commission revenue per sale is modest at the £350 price point. The real margin comes after deducting logistics. This is why membership revenue is the primary revenue stream and commissions are secondary. Commissions become meaningful at scale (hundreds of sales per year) or at higher price points.

### 9.4.4 Break-Even Analysis

**Monthly break-even point:** £3,960/month in revenue

**When does Wallspace hit monthly break-even?**

Based on the revenue projection above, monthly revenue crosses £3,960 in **Month 10-11** (revenue of £3,578-£3,990).

However, this assumes the founder takes £2,500/month from the business. If the founder can defer or reduce salary (funded by savings or part-time work), break-even arrives much sooner:

| Scenario | Monthly Costs | Break-Even Month | Artists Needed |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Full costs (£2,500 founder salary)** | £3,960 | Month 10-11 | ~60 active artists |
| **Reduced salary (£1,500)** | £2,960 | Month 7-8 | ~45 active artists |
| **No salary (bootstrapping)** | £1,460 | Month 4-5 | ~25 active artists |

**Artists needed for viability (full cost basis):**

Monthly costs: £3,960
Average membership revenue per artist: £47/mo
Commission + print revenue per artist per month: ~£15 (at scale)
Total revenue per artist per month: ~£62

**Artists needed for monthly break-even: £3,960 / £62 = ~64 active artists**

This is achievable by Month 10-12 based on the growth assumptions above.

## 9.7 Sensitivity Analysis

### What happens if key assumptions change?

| Scenario | Impact on Year 1 Revenue | Impact on Break-Even |
|---|---|---|
| **Artist growth 50% slower (35 artists by Month 12 instead of 70)** | Revenue drops to ~£16,000 | Break-even pushed to Month 16-18 |
| **Artist churn doubles to 10%/mo** | Revenue drops to ~£21,000 | Break-even pushed to Month 13-15 |
| **Zero artwork sales in Year 1** | Revenue drops to ~£23,500 | Break-even pushed to Month 12-13 |
| **Average membership 20% lower (£38 vs £47)** | Revenue drops to ~£24,000 | Break-even pushed to Month 12-14 |
| **All four negative scenarios combined** | Revenue drops to ~£10,000 | Break-even not reached in Year 1 |
| **Artist growth 50% faster (105 artists by Month 12)** | Revenue increases to ~£38,000 | Break-even at Month 7-8 |
| **Average sale price £500 instead of £350** | Revenue increases to ~£31,500 | Break-even at Month 9-10 |

**Key risk:** The biggest risk is not artist churn or low sales; it is slow artist acquisition. If Wallspace cannot consistently recruit 5-6 new artists per month, the model takes significantly longer to reach viability. Artist acquisition must be the primary operational focus for the first 6 months.

**Key insight:** The model is surprisingly resilient to zero sales. Even with no artwork sales at all, membership revenue alone gets Wallspace close to break-even by end of Year 1. Sales commissions and print revenue accelerate the path but are not required for basic viability. This is the strength of the membership model.
