# Section 10: Supply-Side Strategy

## Purpose

This section defines how Wallspace recruits, vets, and manages its artist supply. The quality of the artist roster is the single biggest determinant of whether venues take Wallspace seriously. A curated, high-quality initial cohort sets the brand standard. Oversupply of mediocre work kills credibility faster than undersupply of great work.

---

## 10.1 Strategic Principles

1. **Curation is the product.** Wallspace is not an open marketplace. Every artist on the platform has been selected. This is the core value proposition to venues.
2. **Photographers first.** Photography is the easiest art form for venues to say yes to. It is commercially accessible, reproduction-friendly, and fits a wide range of interiors. Photographers are the beachhead.
3. **Local-first, London-only at launch.** All first-cohort artists must be London-based or able to deliver/install within London. This keeps logistics simple and enables in-person relationships.
4. **Undersupply is better than oversupply.** Launch with 30-50 carefully selected artists. A waitlist creates perceived value. Letting anyone in destroys it.
5. **Commercial readiness matters.** Art quality alone is not enough. Artists must have work that is print-ready, properly sized, priced sensibly, and suitable for commercial display.

## 10.3 First 50 Artist Recruitment Plan

### Phase 1: Seed Cohort (Artists 1-10) | Weeks 1-3

**Objective:** Recruit 10 strong photographers through direct personal outreach. These artists set the quality bar and become case studies.

**Actions:**
1. Founder spends 2 hours daily on Instagram identifying London-based photographers with strong, consistent portfolios (500-15k followers, regular posting, coherent aesthetic).
2. Send 10 personalised DMs per day (see messaging templates below).
3. Simultaneously attend 2-3 photography meetups or open studio events in East/South London.
4. Approach 3-5 photography collectives with partnership proposals.
5. Target conversion: 10 confirmed artists from approximately 80-100 approaches (10-12% conversion).

**Selection bias at this stage:** Over-index on photographers whose work would look exceptional in a cafe or restaurant. Landscapes, architectural photography, food photography, botanical photography, street photography, and portraiture all work well. Abstract or conceptual work is harder in hospitality venues.

### Phase 2: Expand Cohort (Artists 11-30) | Weeks 4-7

**Objective:** Broaden the roster to 30 artists, still photographer-heavy but beginning to include select mixed-media artists (printmakers, illustrators with edition capabilities).

**Actions:**
1. Continue Instagram outreach at 5-8 DMs per day.
2. Attend 2 art fairs or degree shows.
3. Launch a simple application page (Typeform) and promote through artist communities.
4. Ask Phase 1 artists for 2-3 referrals each.
5. Post in 5-8 online communities with a clear "apply to join" call to action.
6. Visit 3-5 local galleries and ask for introductions.

### Phase 3: Full Launch Roster (Artists 31-50) | Weeks 8-12

**Objective:** Fill remaining slots with a mix of photographers and select non-photography artists. Close applications for founding cohort.

**Actions:**
1. Process inbound applications from Typeform (expect 50-100 applications by this stage).
2. Targeted outreach to fill specific gaps (e.g., if no landscape photographer, or no artist doing large-format work).
3. Activate referral programme formally: one month free membership per successful referral.
4. Create "founding artist" deadline urgency: "Applications for founding membership close [date]."

| Responded positively | 120-160 | 40% |

## 10.6 Application Review Framework (Scoring Rubric)

Every application is scored across five dimensions. Each dimension is scored 1-5. Minimum total score for acceptance: 16/25.

### Dimension 1: Portfolio Quality (1-5)

| Score | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 1 | Inconsistent quality, poor technique, no coherent body of work |
| 2 | Some decent individual pieces but no consistent standard or vision |
| 3 | Competent work with a recognisable style; would look acceptable in a venue |
| 4 | Strong portfolio with clear artistic voice; work that would enhance a venue's atmosphere |
| 5 | Exceptional work; distinctive vision; gallery-level quality adapted for commercial spaces |

### Dimension 2: Commercial Readiness (1-5)

| Score | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 1 | No available work; no prints; no pricing; purely conceptual or digital-only |
| 2 | Some physical work exists but not print-ready; no clear pricing; limited editions unclear |
| 3 | Has prints or can produce them; pricing exists but may need guidance; reasonable edition sizes |
| 4 | Professional prints/framing available; clear pricing strategy; understands editions and reproduction |
| 5 | Fully production-ready; multiple sizes available; professional framing; clear price list; delivery capability |

### Dimension 3: Venue Suitability (1-5)

| Score | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 1 | Work is too niche, controversial, or impractical for commercial display |
| 2 | Some pieces could work in venues but overall portfolio is a stretch |
| 3 | Solid match for at least one venue type (e.g., cafes, restaurants) |
| 4 | Work is versatile across multiple venue types; strong interior design appeal |
| 5 | Work would be actively sought by venues; transforms commercial spaces |

### Dimension 4: Professionalism and Reliability (1-5)

| Score | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 1 | Unresponsive; incomplete application; no online presence |
| 2 | Slow to respond; minimal effort in application; unclear availability |
| 3 | Responsive; complete application; adequate online presence |
| 4 | Prompt, professional communication; strong online presence; clear about availability and logistics |
| 5 | Exceptional communication; existing track record of exhibitions or commercial work; highly organised |

### Dimension 5: Strategic Value (1-5)

| Score | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 1 | Duplicates existing roster exactly; no differentiation |
| 2 | Minor differentiation from current roster |
| 3 | Adds a useful new style, medium, or price point |
| 4 | Fills a clear gap in the roster (e.g., first landscape photographer, first large-format artist) |
| 5 | Brings unique value: strong following, press coverage, existing venue relationships, or exceptional niche |

### Decision Matrix

| Total Score | Decision |
|---|---|
| 20-25 | Accept immediately; fast-track onboarding |
| 16-19 | Accept; standard onboarding |
| 12-15 | Waitlist; provide feedback on what would strengthen application |
| Below 12 | Decline with encouragement to reapply when portfolio develops |

## 10.7 Quality Signals vs. Weak-Fit Signals

### Strong Quality Signals

- Consistent visual style across portfolio (not random collection of unrelated images)
- Work has been exhibited, published, or sold before
- Professional presentation: proper framing, clean prints, considered sizing
- Active Instagram or portfolio site updated within last 3 months
- Pricing that reflects market awareness (not wildly over- or under-priced)
- Can articulate their artistic direction in a few sentences
- Responds promptly and professionally to outreach
- Has physical prints available or a clear production pipeline
- London-based or can credibly deliver/install within London

### Weak-Fit / Spam Signals

- Portfolio is a grab-bag of unrelated styles and subjects
- Only digital work exists; no print production experience or plan
- Pricing is either absent, wildly unrealistic (too high or too low), or copy-pasted from a template
- No online presence beyond the application
- Work is heavily filtered or relies entirely on post-processing effects
- Cannot articulate why they want work in commercial spaces (just wants "exposure")
- Overly aggressive follow-up or entitlement about acceptance
- Application is generic or copy-pasted (no personalisation)
- Located outside London with no logistics plan
- Work contains themes unsuitable for commercial display (graphic violence, explicit content, highly polarising political imagery) without awareness of context

## 10.8 Preventing Oversupply

### Hard Caps

- **Launch cap:** 50 artists maximum in founding cohort.
- **Ongoing cap:** No more than 10 new artists per month after launch.
- **Category caps:** No more than 30% of roster in any single genre (e.g., maximum 15 street photographers out of 50).

### Waitlist Mechanics

- Once caps are reached, new applicants join a waitlist.
- Waitlisted artists are notified monthly on status.
- Waitlist priority is based on application score and roster gaps.
- Waitlist creates urgency: "We're accepting 10 more artists this quarter."

### Roster Health Monitoring

- Track per-artist metrics monthly: number of placements offered, number accepted, sales generated, venue feedback.
- Artists with zero placements after 3 months get a check-in call. If work is not matching venues, discuss adjustments or consider whether the fit is right.
- Artists who cancel membership or go inactive for 60+ days are removed from active roster. Their slot opens for new recruitment.

### Quality Floor Maintenance

- Annual portfolio review for all roster artists.
- If an artist's new work falls below the original acceptance standard, have a conversation about it.
- Venues can flag quality concerns; any artist flagged by 2+ venues gets a portfolio review.

## 10.9 Building Curated Identity from Day One

### Brand Positioning for Artists

Wallspace is not a place to dump unsold work. It is a selective programme that places excellent art in excellent spaces. Every communication reinforces this:

- Application language: "Apply to join" not "Sign up."
- Acceptance language: "You've been selected for our roster" not "Your account is active."
- Rejection language: "We're not able to offer a place at this time" not "Your application was unsuccessful."
- Waitlist language: "We'd love to include you when a space opens" not "You're on a waiting list."

### Curation Principles (Published to Artists and Venues)

1. Every artist on Wallspace has been individually reviewed and selected.
2. We look for distinctive vision, technical competence, and commercial readiness.
3. We maintain a deliberately small roster to ensure every artist gets meaningful placement opportunities.
4. We match work to spaces carefully -- not every piece suits every venue, and that specificity is the point.

### Early Wins to Publicise

- Photograph every installation. Build an Instagram feed that shows the work in situ -- this is Wallspace's most powerful marketing asset.
- Get a short quote from every participating venue and artist after first placement.
- Feature one "Artist Spotlight" per week on Instagram/newsletter once launch cohort is active.

## 10.10 Photographer-First Strategy: Rationale and Execution

### Why Photographers First

1. **Volume of available work.** Photographers typically have larger catalogues than painters or sculptors. One photographer might have 20-50 pieces available immediately.
2. **Reproduction capability.** Photographs can be printed in multiple sizes and editions. If a piece sells, it can be replaced quickly.
3. **Price accessibility.** Photographic prints typically range from 50-500, which sits in the sweet spot for impulse or considered purchases in a cafe/restaurant context.
4. **Venue comfort.** Venues are more comfortable saying yes to photography than to paintings or mixed media. Photography feels less "risky" and more universally appealing.
5. **Instagram alignment.** Photographers already market on Instagram. They understand visual platforms and can help promote Wallspace placements to their followers.

### When to Expand Beyond Photography

- After first 20 placements are live and running smoothly.
- When venue feedback indicates appetite for different media (paintings, prints, illustrations).
- When the operational workflow (installation, removal, rotation) is proven and stable.
- Target: begin accepting non-photography artists from month 3-4.

## 10.10 Quality Control SOP

### Pre-Acceptance

1. **Application received** via Typeform. Auto-confirmation email sent.
2. **Initial screen** (same day or next day): Check for completeness. Reject incomplete applications with a request to resubmit.
3. **Portfolio review** (within 5 working days): Score application using the 5-dimension rubric. Two reviewers if possible (founder + one trusted early artist or advisor).
4. **Decision:** Accept, waitlist, or decline. Communicate within 7 days of submission.
5. **Onboarding call** (15-20 minutes): Walk through how Wallspace works, confirm logistics, discuss which pieces to list first, set expectations on timeline.

### Post-Acceptance (Ongoing)

1. **Monthly check-in (automated):** Email each artist with their stats: pieces listed, venues offered, placements active, sales, total earnings.
2. **Quarterly portfolio refresh:** Ask artists to submit any new work. Review and add to their listing.
3. **Venue feedback loop:** After every placement, collect brief venue feedback (1-5 rating on quality, presentation, customer response). Share positive feedback with artist. Address concerns directly.
4. **Issue resolution:** If venue reports damage, poor presentation, or customer complaints about a piece, contact artist within 24 hours. Investigate, resolve, document.
5. **Annual review:** Full portfolio and performance review. Confirm continued fit with the roster. Discuss any changes to pricing, availability, or artistic direction.

### Removal Criteria

An artist may be removed from the roster if:
- They are unresponsive for 30+ days to placement-related communications.
- Two or more venues report quality or professionalism concerns.
- They have had zero active placements for 6+ months despite being offered opportunities.
- Their membership payments lapse for 60+ days.
- Their work quality has materially declined from the standard at which they were accepted.

Removal is communicated personally by the founder (phone or video call preferred, email as fallback). The door is left open for reapplication after 6 months.
