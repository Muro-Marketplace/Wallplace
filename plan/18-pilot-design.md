# Section 18: Pilot Design

## Pilot Objective

Validate the core Wallspace model in the real world: can a concierge-managed art placement service attract quality artists willing to pay a monthly membership, secure venue partners willing to display the work, and generate enough traction (engagement, enquiries, sales) to justify scaling?

The pilot is not about revenue maximisation. It is about proving the unit economics, testing operational assumptions, and building the reference base needed to expand with confidence.

---

## What Type of Venues to Test With First

### Primary Target: Independent Cafes

Cafes are the ideal pilot venue for five reasons:

1. **Wall availability.** Most independent cafes have 2-4 walls with minimal decoration. The real estate is literally sitting there unused.
2. **Dwell time.** Cafe customers sit for 20-60 minutes. They look at the walls. They notice art. This is fundamentally different from a takeaway or a fast-food chain where no one lingers.
3. **Decision-making speed.** Independent cafes are typically owner-operated. One conversation, one decision. No committee, no head office, no brand guidelines committee.
4. **Community orientation.** Independent cafe owners care about their space and their neighbourhood. Supporting local artists is genuinely aligned with their values, not a corporate CSR exercise.
5. **Volume.** London has thousands of independent cafes. There is no shortage of targets in the launch geography.

### Secondary Target: Independent Restaurants

Restaurants have longer dwell time and higher-value clientele (people spending £30-80 on dinner are more likely to spend £200-500 on art). However, restaurants are harder to access (busier owners, more design-conscious, potential resistance to changing established interiors). Include 2-3 restaurants in the pilot but do not rely on them.

### Tertiary Target: Wine Bars and Cocktail Bars

Excellent aesthetics, high-spending clientele, owners who care about ambience. Include 1-2 if the opportunity arises. Downside: evening-only operation makes installation and relationship management slightly harder.

### Avoid in Pilot:
- **Chain venues.** Decision-making is centralised and slow. Not worth the effort for a pilot.
- **Co-working spaces.** Different dynamic -- members, not customers. Art is less noticed. Save for later.
- **Venues with highly branded interiors.** If the walls are already part of a design concept, adding external art may conflict. Target venues with relatively neutral or unfinished walls.

---

## Pilot Scale

### Venues: 10-15

- **Minimum viable:** 10 venues. Fewer than 10 does not produce enough data to draw conclusions or enough social proof to recruit artists.
- **Target:** 12-15 venues. Enough variety to test different space types, neighbourhoods, and clienteles without overwhelming a single operator.
- **Maximum:** 15. Beyond this, the founder cannot personally manage every relationship, every installation, and every rotation in the pilot phase.

### Geography: Concentrated

All pilot venues within a 30-minute cycle of each other. This is critical for logistics:

- **Primary cluster:** Peckham / Camberwell / Bermondsey (3-5 venues)
- **Secondary cluster:** Hackney / Dalston / Bethnal Green (3-5 venues)
- **Tertiary:** Brixton / Deptford / Lewisham (2-4 venues)

Geographic concentration reduces transport time, enables walk-in relationship maintenance, and creates visible neighbourhood density that artists and venues can reference.

### Artists: 30-50

- **Minimum:** 30 artists. This provides enough variety to match different venue aesthetics and enough critical mass for the founding cohort narrative.
- **Target:** 40 artists. Allows for some natural attrition (artists who sign up but are slow to deliver work, or who churn in month 1-2).
- **Maximum:** 50. Beyond this, the founder cannot personally manage portfolio reviews, matching, and relationship maintenance.
- **Breakdown by medium:** 60-70% photographers (easier to produce display-ready work, more consistent output), 30-40% mixed media (painters, printmakers, illustrators -- adds variety and higher price points).

## Art Formats to Include

### Required Formats

- **Framed photographic prints.** The backbone of the pilot. Easiest to produce, transport, install, and swap. Standard sizes (A4, A3, A2) in simple frames.
- **Mounted photographic prints.** Foam-mounted or aluminium-mounted prints for a more modern look. No frame needed.
- **Framed original paintings or drawings.** Higher price points, more variety, stronger visual impact. Must be securely framed.
- **Framed prints (limited edition).** Giclée or screen prints in editions. Clear edition numbering. Good margin for artists.

### Optional Formats (Test 2-3)

- **Canvas wraps.** No framing needed. Lower barrier for some artists. Test whether venues and customers respond.
- **Small sculptural pieces.** Only for venues with shelf or display surface space. High risk of damage but high visual impact and high price points.

### Excluded from Pilot

- **Oversized work.** Anything above A1. Logistics and wall space constraints make this impractical for most cafes.
- **Unframed or unfinished work.** Everything must be display-ready. No "we will frame it later" -- this causes delays and looks unprofessional.
- **Digital screens or projections.** Not in scope. Wallspace is about physical art.
- **Extremely fragile or high-value work.** Pieces over £2,000 or requiring special climate control are not suitable for a cafe wall. Save for premium venues later.

## Exact Offer to Test

### For Venues

- Free curated art placement
- Professional installation
- Rotation every 6-8 weeks
- 10% revenue share on any sales from their venue
- No contract, no commitment beyond one rotation cycle
- Full approval rights on all artwork before installation

### For Artists

- Managed placement in 1 venue (Founding tier)
- Professional installation and rotation
- Sales support (QR codes, online profile, enquiry handling)
- 80/20 sales split (artist keeps 80%)
- Founding rate: £29/month, locked permanently

### Pricing Decision: Should Artists Pay Immediately?

**Yes, but with a risk-reduction mechanism.**

Artists should pay from month 1. The founding rate of £29/month is deliberately low enough to minimise resistance, and immediate payment is critical for two reasons:

1. **Revenue validation.** If artists will not pay £29/month for placement, the model does not work. Better to discover this in the pilot than after scaling.
2. **Commitment filter.** Artists who pay deliver work on time, respond to messages, and take the service seriously. Free pilots attract people who ghost.

**Risk-reduction mechanism:** Offer a money-back guarantee on the first month. If the artist is not placed in a venue within 30 days of joining, they get a full refund. This removes the downside risk while preserving the commitment signal.

**Alternative considered and rejected:** A free pilot period (e.g., first month free, then £29/month). This delays revenue validation, attracts uncommitted artists, and creates an awkward conversion point. The founding rate is already cheap enough to function as its own trial.

## How to Measure Traction

### Primary Traction Metrics

| Metric | What it tells you | How to measure |
|---|---|---|
| **Artist sign-up rate** | Is the offer compelling enough for artists to pay? | Applications received / accepted / converted to paying members |
| **Artist retention (month 2+)** | Is the service delivering enough value to retain? | % of founding artists still paying after month 2, month 3 |
| **Venue acceptance rate** | How easy is it to get venues to say yes? | Venues pitched / venues that agreed / venues with art installed |
| **Customer engagement** | Are venue customers noticing and interacting with the art? | QR code scans per venue per week |
| **Sales enquiries** | Are people asking about buying? | Enquiries received (via QR, email, staff) per venue per month |
| **Actual sales** | Is the sales mechanism working? | Number of pieces sold, total revenue, average sale price |

### Secondary Traction Metrics

| Metric | What it tells you |
|---|---|
| **Venue satisfaction** | Do venues want to continue after the first rotation? |
| **Artist satisfaction** | Are artists happy with placements, communication, and service? |
| **Time per venue** | How long does it take to manage each venue relationship? |
| **Time per artist** | How long does it take to manage each artist relationship? |
| **Installation time** | How long does a typical installation take (including transport)? |
| **Rotation logistics** | How long does a full rotation cycle take across all venues? |
| **Social media mentions** | Are venues or customers posting about the art? |
| **Inbound interest** | Are venues or artists contacting Wallspace without outreach? |

## What Data to Collect

### Per Venue

- Venue name, address, neighbourhood, type (cafe/restaurant/bar)
- Number of walls available, total linear metres of display space
- Number of pieces displayed
- Customer footfall (ask owner for estimate; verify with observation)
- QR code scan count (weekly)
- Sales enquiries received (weekly)
- Sales completed (ongoing)
- Venue owner satisfaction (monthly check-in, 1-5 rating + open feedback)
- Staff engagement with art (do they mention it to customers? monthly check)
- Time spent on venue management per week (founder tracks)
- Photos of installation (for portfolio and marketing)

### Per Artist

- Name, medium, price range, tier
- Number of pieces submitted, number accepted for display
- Number of pieces currently displayed, across how many venues
- Sales: number sold, revenue, average price
- QR code scans on their work (if trackable per artist)
- Artist satisfaction (monthly check-in, 1-5 rating + open feedback)
- Time from acceptance to first placement
- Communication responsiveness (qualitative)
- Work quality consistency (qualitative)
- Time spent on artist management per week (founder tracks)

### Per Sale

- Artwork title, artist, medium, size
- Sale price
- Venue where sold
- How the buyer found it (QR scan, asked staff, other)
- Time from installation to sale
- Commission paid (Wallspace and venue)
- Artist payment date and amount
- Buyer feedback (if available)

### Operational Data

- Total founder hours per week on: curation, artist management, venue management, installation/logistics, sales support, admin, marketing
- Cost per installation (transport, hardware, time)
- Cost per rotation cycle (full swap across all venues)
- Total monthly operating costs vs. total monthly revenue
- Cash flow by week

## What Success Looks Like

### Hard Success Criteria (Must Hit)

| Metric | Target | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Paying artists at end of pilot** | 25+ (out of 30-50 recruited) | Proves willingness to pay. 80%+ retention through pilot. |
| **Venues with art installed** | 10+ | Proves venue acquisition is feasible at target rate. |
| **Venue continuation rate** | 80%+ agree to second rotation | Proves venue satisfaction and service quality. |
| **QR code scans** | 50+ total per month across all venues | Proves customer engagement with the art. |
| **Sales enquiries** | 10+ per month across all venues | Proves buying intent exists. |
| **Actual sales** | 3+ pieces sold in pilot period | Proves the sales mechanism works, even at low volume. |
| **Monthly membership revenue** | £800+ per month by end of pilot | 25+ artists at £29/mo avg = £725+. Shows revenue foundation. |
| **Founder time manageable** | Sub-50 hours/week | Proves one person can run 10-15 venues and 30-50 artists. |

### Soft Success Indicators (Strong Signals)

- Venues asking when the next rotation is happening (pull, not push)
- Artists referring other artists to the programme
- Venue customers posting about the art on social media (organic, not prompted)
- Inbound enquiries from venues or artists who heard about Wallspace through word of mouth
- At least one venue owner offering to connect you with other venue owners
- At least one artist upgrading from Founding to Core tier
- Local press or blog coverage without paid promotion
- A venue customer buying a second piece from the same or different artist

## What Failure Looks Like

### Hard Failure Signals (Model May Not Work)

| Signal | What it means | Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| **Artist sign-ups below 20** | The offer is not compelling at £29/mo | Fewer than 20 paying artists after 6 weeks of outreach |
| **Artist churn above 40% in month 2** | Artists do not see enough value to continue paying | 40%+ cancel after month 1 |
| **Venue rejection rate above 70%** | The free offer is not compelling enough, or the pitch is wrong | Fewer than 30% of pitched venues agree |
| **Zero QR scans after 4 weeks** | Customers are not noticing or engaging with the art | 0 scans across all venues for a full month |
| **Zero sales enquiries after 8 weeks** | No buying intent exists in these venues | 0 enquiries across all venues over 2 months |
| **Venues asking for art removal** | The service is creating problems, not solving them | 2+ venues requesting removal within the first rotation |
| **Founder time exceeding 60 hours/week** | Operations are not scalable even at pilot scale | Sustained overwork with no path to reduction |

### Ambiguous Signals (Investigate, Do Not Panic)

- Low scan counts but positive venue feedback -- may indicate QR code placement or visibility issues, not lack of interest
- No sales but high scan counts -- may indicate pricing issues, not demand issues
- High artist interest but low conversion to payment -- may indicate pricing or trust issues, not market rejection
- Venue enthusiasm but slow installation scheduling -- may indicate operational bottleneck, not venue reluctance

## When to Iterate vs. Continue

### Continue As-Is When:

- Metrics are within 20% of target on most dimensions
- Feedback from artists and venues is broadly positive
- Operational workload is sustainable
- No single failure signal is triggered
- Month-over-month trends are flat or improving

### Iterate (Change Something Specific) When:

- One or two metrics are significantly below target but others are healthy
- Clear feedback identifies a specific fixable problem (pricing, placement quality, communication frequency, QR code visibility)
- Operational bottleneck is identified and can be solved with a process change

**Examples of iteration:**
- QR scans are low: test different placement (eye level vs. beside the piece), different formats (NFC tag, printed card on table), staff prompts
- Artist churn is high: survey churned artists, adjust onboarding, improve communication frequency, add more venue options
- Sales enquiries but no conversions: test pricing adjustments, add limited-time offers, improve checkout flow
- Venue owners feel uninformed: add a monthly email update, create a WhatsApp group, schedule brief check-in calls

### Pivot (Reconsider a Core Assumption) When:

- Multiple hard failure signals triggered simultaneously
- 8+ weeks of iteration produce no improvement in failing metrics
- Founder is working unsustainable hours with no operational path to improvement
- Artist willingness to pay is fundamentally absent (fewer than 15 paying artists after exhaustive outreach)
- Venue model requires payment to achieve acceptable quality (free model attracts wrong venues)

**Pivots to consider:**
- Commission-only model (drop membership, increase commission to 30-40%)
- Venue-pays model (flip the revenue side -- charge venues, let artists list free)
- B2B art consultancy (drop the marketplace layer, sell curation as a service to venues)
- Event-based model (pop-up exhibitions rather than permanent placement)
- Geographic pivot (different city or neighbourhood if London market assumptions are wrong)

## Pilot Structure

### Pre-Pilot: Weeks -4 to 0

**Objective:** Recruit founding artists and secure pilot venues before a single piece of art goes on a wall.

| Week | Focus | Targets |
|---|---|---|
| **Week -4** | Artist outreach begins. Cold emails, DMs, portfolio reviews. Launch founding artist page. | 50+ artists contacted, 20+ applications received |
| **Week -3** | Continue artist outreach. Begin venue outreach in primary cluster (Peckham/Camberwell/Bermondsey). Walk-ins, emails, DMs. | 30+ venues contacted, 10+ conversations had |
| **Week -2** | Artist portfolio reviews and acceptance. First founding artists confirmed and paying. Venue walk-throughs and proposal prep. | 15+ artists accepted and paying, 8+ venue proposals sent |
| **Week -1** | Finalise first rotation. Match artists to venues. Confirm installation schedule. Prepare all artwork for transport and installation. | 10+ venues confirmed, 25+ artists paying, first rotation planned |

### Phase 1: Installation and Launch (Weeks 1-2)

**Objective:** Get art on walls. Prove the operational model works.

| Week | Focus | Targets |
|---|---|---|
| **Week 1** | Install in first 5-7 venues. Professional installation, QR codes live, price cards placed. Document everything. | 5-7 venues installed, all QR codes functional, all artists notified of placements |
| **Week 2** | Install in remaining venues. First check-ins with Week 1 venues. Monitor QR scan data. | 10-15 total venues installed, first scan data collected, first venue feedback received |

### Phase 2: Observation and Optimisation (Weeks 3-6)

**Objective:** Collect data. Identify problems. Fix what is fixable. Build relationships.

| Week | Focus | Targets |
|---|---|---|
| **Week 3** | First full week of data collection. Weekly venue check-ins begin. Monitor scans, enquiries, feedback. | Scan data for all venues, first enquiry data, first artist feedback |
| **Week 4** | Artist check-ins. Gather feedback on placement quality, communication, and satisfaction. Address any operational issues. | All artists contacted, satisfaction scores collected, operational issues logged |
| **Week 5** | Venue satisfaction check. Identify any venues needing artwork swaps or adjustments. Assess scan and enquiry trends. | Venue satisfaction scores, trend analysis on scans and enquiries, first sales (target) |
| **Week 6** | Mid-pilot review. Compile data. Assess against KPIs. Decide: continue, iterate, or adjust. | Full data review completed, iteration plan if needed |

### Phase 3: First Rotation (Weeks 7-8)

**Objective:** Test the rotation model. Prove the service is ongoing, not one-off.

| Week | Focus | Targets |
|---|---|---|
| **Week 7** | Plan and execute first rotation across all venues. New work installed, old work collected. | All venues rotated, new QR codes live, artists notified |
| **Week 8** | Post-rotation check-ins. Measure venue response to fresh work. Monitor artist retention (are month-2 payments coming through?). | Rotation complete, month 2 retention measured, venue continuation confirmed |

### Phase 4: Pilot Review (Weeks 9-10)

**Objective:** Assess the pilot against all success criteria. Decide whether to scale, iterate, or pivot.

| Week | Focus | Targets |
|---|---|---|
| **Week 9** | Full data compilation. Artist and venue satisfaction surveys. Operational cost accounting. | All data compiled, surveys completed, cost model updated |
| **Week 10** | Pilot retrospective. Decision: scale, iterate, or pivot. Document learnings. Plan next phase. | Retrospective completed, decision made, next phase planned |

## Pilot Success Criteria

The pilot is successful if the following conditions are met at the end of Week 10:

### Must-Have (All Required)

1. **25+ artists paying £29/month** with 80%+ retained through month 2
2. **10+ venues with art installed** and 80%+ agreeing to continue with a second rotation
3. **At least 1 sale completed** through the Wallspace sales mechanism
4. **QR code engagement above zero** in at least 8 of the installed venues
5. **Founder workload sustainable** at under 50 hours per week with a clear path to operational improvement

### Should-Have (3 of 5 Required)

1. 3+ sales completed in the pilot period
2. At least 2 inbound enquiries from venues or artists who found Wallspace without direct outreach
3. Artist satisfaction score of 4.0+ out of 5
4. Venue satisfaction score of 4.0+ out of 5
5. At least one artist upgrade from Founding to Core tier

### Nice-to-Have (Signals of Exceptional Traction)

- 5+ sales in pilot period
- Organic press or blog coverage
- Artist or venue waitlist forming
- Venue owner referring another venue owner
- Customer buying two or more pieces

## Pilot Retrospective Framework

At the end of Week 10, conduct a structured retrospective covering the following areas.

### 1. Model Validation

- **Is the two-sided model working?** Are both artists and venues getting value? Is either side significantly harder to serve than expected?
- **Is the pricing right?** Are artists balking at £29/month? Would they pay more? Are venues expecting payment for wall space?
- **Is the commission model working?** Were there sales? Was the 80/20 split accepted by artists? Did venues care about the 10% share?
- **Is the concierge model sustainable?** Can one person realistically manage 10-15 venues and 30-50 artists? What breaks first?

### 2. Demand Signals

- **Artist demand:** How many applied? How many converted? How many churned? Why did they churn? What did they value most?
- **Venue demand:** How easy was it to get venues to say yes? What was the most common objection? What sealed the deal?
- **Customer demand:** Are customers engaging with the art? Are they buying? What price points work? What formats and styles get the most attention?

### 3. Operational Learnings

- **What took longer than expected?** (Installation, rotation, artist onboarding, venue onboarding, sales handling)
- **What was easier than expected?**
- **What broke?** (Logistics failures, miscommunications, artwork damage, payment issues)
- **What processes need to be built or improved?** (Tracking, invoicing, scheduling, communication templates)
- **What tools or systems are needed?** (CRM, scheduling, inventory management, financial tracking)

### 4. Financial Review

- **Total revenue** (membership + commission)
- **Total costs** (transport, materials, insurance, tools, time valued at target hourly rate)
- **Unit economics:** Revenue per artist, cost per artist, revenue per venue, cost per venue
- **Path to profitability:** At what scale does revenue exceed costs? Is that scale achievable?
- **Cash flow:** When did money come in? When did costs hit? Any timing issues?

### 5. Scaling Assessment

- **What needs to change before scaling from 15 venues to 30?** (People, processes, tools, funding)
- **What is the bottleneck?** (Venue acquisition, artist acquisition, logistics, sales, founder time)
- **What is the first hire?** (Operations/logistics assistant, sales, marketing, another curator)
- **What is the next geography?** (Expand within East/South London, or add North/West?)
- **What is the next segment?** (Co-working spaces, hotels, offices, retail?)

### 6. Decision Matrix

Based on the retrospective, select one of three paths:

| Path | When to choose |
|---|---|
| **Scale** | All must-have criteria met. 3+ should-have criteria met. Operational model is sustainable. Clear path to 30+ venues. |
| **Iterate** | Most must-have criteria met but 1-2 are borderline. Specific, fixable problems identified. Run a second 8-week cycle with adjustments before scaling. |
| **Pivot** | Multiple must-have criteria missed. Core model assumptions disproven. Redirect to alternative model (commission-only, B2B consultancy, event-based, or geographic pivot). |

Document the decision, the evidence supporting it, and the specific plan for the next 90 days.
