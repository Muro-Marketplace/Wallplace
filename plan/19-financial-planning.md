## 19.3 Revenue Model — Detailed Assumptions

### Membership Revenue

| Tier | Monthly Price | Expected Mix (Steady State) |
|------|--------------|---------------------------|
| Founding | £29 | 30% (early adopters, grandfathered) |
| Core | £49 | 50% (default tier) |
| Premium | £89 | 20% (power users) |

**Blended average revenue per artist: ~£49/month** (weighted by tier mix).

Early months will skew toward Founding tier (£29–35 blended average) as early adopters get the lower rate.

### Transaction Revenue (Commission)

Assumptions:
- Average artwork sale price: £350 (range £200–500)
- Commission rate: 20% on customer sales, 15% on direct venue purchases
- Blended commission rate: ~18% (most sales are customer-initiated)
- Average commission per sale: £63

Sales velocity assumptions:
- Months 1–3: 0.5 sales per active venue per month (venues are new, footfall building)
- Months 4–6: 1.0 sale per active venue per month
- Months 7–12: 1.5 sales per active venue per month (some venues higher, some lower)

## 19.7 Cash Flow Considerations

### Cash Flow Positive Characteristics
- **Membership revenue is pre-paid**: Artists pay at the start of the month, costs are incurred throughout. This creates a natural positive cash flow cycle.
- **Low inventory risk**: Wallspace never owns the art. No capital tied up in stock.
- **Low CAPEX**: No warehouse, no fleet, no manufacturing equipment.

### Cash Flow Risks
- **Seasonality**: Art sales may dip in January/February and peak November/December (Christmas buying). Membership revenue provides a stabilising baseline.
- **Churn spikes**: If 10+ artists leave in the same month (e.g., after a price increase or poor experience), revenue drops sharply while costs remain fixed.
- **Commission timing**: Sales commission is collected at point of sale, but if using an escrow or delayed settlement process, there may be a 7–14 day lag.
- **Installation costs front-loaded**: When onboarding a batch of new venues, installation costs spike before those venues generate any sales.
- **VAT threshold**: At £90,000 turnover (which the high case approaches by month 12), VAT registration becomes mandatory. This effectively reduces margin by up to 20% on membership fees unless passed on to artists.

### Cash Reserve Recommendation
Maintain a minimum cash buffer of 2 months' operating costs at all times:
- Months 1–6: £3,000–4,000 buffer
- Months 7–12: £5,000–8,000 buffer

## 19.8 Likely Financial Bottlenecks

### 1. Artist Acquisition Cost vs. Lifetime Value
- If it costs more than £30–50 to acquire an artist (paid channels), and average retention is less than 6 months, unit economics break.
- **Mitigation**: Keep acquisition organic (Instagram, referrals, community) for as long as possible. Target CAC under £20.

### 2. Churn
- If monthly churn exceeds 8–10%, the business is on a treadmill — constantly replacing artists just to maintain revenue.
- **Mitigation**: Deliver tangible value (venue placements, sales) within the first 60 days. Artists who get placed churn at far lower rates.

### 3. Sales Volume Dependency
- Commission revenue requires actual artwork sales. If venues don't sell, the commission engine stalls.
- **Mitigation**: Membership revenue provides the baseline. Commission is upside. Don't build the financial model on commission alone.

### 4. Founder Time as the Constraint
- The founder is doing sales, operations, installation, marketing, and finance. There are only so many hours in the week.
- **Mitigation**: Ruthlessly prioritise. Automate admin early. Hire the first part-time role (art handler or ops assistant) as soon as monthly revenue consistently exceeds £4,000.

### 5. Venue Density Economics
- If venues are spread across London, travel time for installations and rotations eats into the founder's capacity.
- **Mitigation**: Cluster venues geographically. Start with a tight radius (e.g., Peckham to Bermondsey corridor) and expand outward.

- £6,000 startup costs
- £5,000 operating runway (first 3 months before revenue builds)
- £4,000–9,000 contingency and modest founder income
